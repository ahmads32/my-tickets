Good morning,

What do you like most about living in Canada? In response to a recent national poll, residents number one answer was health care.

Canadians are justifiably proud of medicare. But U of T Mississauga anthropology professor Tracey Galloway has found that not everyone experiences it the same way – especially Inuit cancer patients in Canada’s Far North. Treatment requires travelling far from home, where people “are days away from that hug or from sitting together on the couch dealing with the news,” says Galloway.

In this issue, we also explore relatively uncharted territory from our nation’s past. Deputy editor Stacey Gibson recounts the life of Dr. Elizabeth Bagshaw, who became medical director of Canada’s first (and illegal) birth control clinic in 1932 – risking prison to help women control their reproductive health and plan the size of their families. U of T history professor Laurie Bertram tells the unlikely story of a brothel-keeper in 1880s Edmonton who stood trial for shooting a Mountie (in self-defence) – and walked free. And although Black people have lived on the Prairies since before Canada began, their stories have been mostly erased, says U of T Scarborough English professor Karina Vernon. She speaks about this lost literature – and her attempt to restore it.

Finally, we explore the unique way Canadians talk, investigate how Canada could lead the world in eliminating bias from artificial intelligence, and take a fresh look at 10 Canadian icons and their connections to U of T.

Happy reading,


Scott Anderson
Editor, University of Toronto Magazine
